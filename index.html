Section 2

Definition of Optic Neuritis

  • Optic Neuritis (ON): Inflammatory demyelination of the optic nerve
  • Presentation: Subacute onset of unilateral vision loss, eye pain
  • Significance: Often the first manifestation of MS
Section 2

Importance of Studying ON in MS

  • ON is often the first demyelinating event in MS
  • ~15–20% of MS patients initially present with ON
  • Provides insight into autoimmune demyelination in the CNS.
  • Early detection can prompt disease-modifying therapies
Section 3

Epidemiology

  • Age: Typically 20–40 years
  • Gender: Female predominance (~3:1)
  • Geographic Variation: Higher incidence farther from the equator
  • Association: 50% of isolated ON develop MS within 15 years
Section 3

Risk Factors

  • Genetic Predisposition: Certain HLA genotypes
  • Environmental Factors:
    • Low Vitamin D
    • Smoking
    • Viral triggers (EBV association)
  • Autoimmune Disorders: May predispose to ON
Section 4

Basic Immunopathology

  • Autoimmune Attack: T-cells, B-cells, and antibodies target myelin
  • Key Cells: CD4+, CD8+ T cells, B cells, macrophages
  • Inflammatory Cascade: Cytokines (IL-2, IFN-γ) promote demyelination, axonal injury
Section 4

Demyelination in the Optic Nerve

  • Myelin Loss: Slows nerve conduction
  • Axonal Injury: Potentially permanent if severe
  • Remyelination: Explains partial or full visual recovery.
Section 4

Role of MS Plaques

  • MS Plaques: Focal CNS demyelination
  • Locations: Periventricular, juxtacortical, infratentorial, spinal cord
  • MRI: T2/FLAIR lesions; active lesions enhance with gadolinium
  • Optic Nerve Lesions: Enhancement indicates acute ON inflammation.
Section 5

Typical vs. Atypical ON

  • Typical (Demyelinating) ON:
    • Often associated with MS
    • Usually unilateral, painful, better recovery
    • MRI often shows MS-like lesions
  • Atypical ON:
    • Causes: NMO, MOG, infections, sarcoidosis
    • Often bilateral or severe; may have worse prognosis
Section 5

Clinical Clues to Atypical ON

  • Poor steroid response
  • Recurrent bilateral involvement
  • Severe vision loss at onset
  • Systemic symptoms (rash, arthralgias)
  • Marked disc swelling on imaging
Section 5

ON as an Early Indicator of MS

  • 15–20% of MS patients present with ON initially
  • ~50% develop MS within 15 years after first ON
  • MRI brain lesions greatly increase MS conversion risk
Section 6

MS Diagnostic Criteria (Brief)

  • McDonald Criteria: Dissemination in time & space.
  • Clinical Attacks + MRI Evidence:
    • ≥2 CNS regions (space)
    • Lesions at different times (time)
  • Optic Neuritis can fulfill part of the clinical criteria.
Section 6

Vision Loss

  • Nature: Sudden or subacute onset over days to ~1 week
  • Severity: Mild blurring to complete vision loss
  • Laterality: Typically unilateral in demyelinating ON
  • Progression: Often worsens for ~1 week, then stabilizes/improves
Section 6

Pain with Eye Movement

  • ~90% of patients report orbital pain, worsened by eye movement
  • Due to inflammation of the optic nerve sheath
Section 6

Color Vision Defects

  • Red desaturation is a hallmark of ON
  • Ishihara plates may show color discrimination deficits
  • Residual color vision changes can persist even after acuity improves
Section 6

Visual Field Defects

  • Most common: Central or cecocentral scotoma
  • Diffuse field loss or altitudinal defect is less frequent
  • Confrontation test (screen), automated perimetry (detailed)
Section 6

Uhthoff’s Phenomenon

  • Worsening of neurological symptoms with heat/exercise
  • Elevated body temperature further impairs conduction in demyelinated axons
  • Can transiently reduce visual acuity
Section 6

Relative Afferent Pupillary Defect (RAPD)

  • Use the swinging flashlight test to detect unilateral/asymmetric dysfunction
  • More pronounced RAPD generally indicates more severe ON involvement
Section 7

Key Points in History (Part 1)

  • Chief complaint: Exact nature & onset of vision loss
  • Pain: Especially with eye movement
  • Timeline: Duration, progression
  • Possible triggers: Recent infection, vaccination, or stress
Section 7

Key Points in History (Part 2)

  • Systemic Review:
    • Neurological: numbness, weakness, ataxia
    • Urinary changes, diplopia
  • Past Medical History:
    • Previous ON episodes
    • Known autoimmune/neuro disorders
  • Family History: MS or other autoimmune diseases
Section 7

Additional History Details

  • Medication Use: steroids, immunomodulators, etc.
  • Social History: smoking, alcohol, occupation
  • Travel History: risk for infections
  • Red Flags: Systemic signs like fever, weight loss, or night sweats
Section 8

Visual Acuity Testing

  • Measure best-corrected VA (Snellen, ETDRS)
  • Compare findings with the contralateral eye
  • Check for pinhole improvement
Section 8

Pupillary Examination

  • Size, shape, symmetry
  • Direct & consensual light reflex
  • Swinging flashlight test (RAPD)
  • Near response (accommodation, convergence)
Section 8

Color Vision Testing

  • Ishihara plates to screen for color defects
  • Red desaturation test for subtle differences
  • Document severity/persistence of color deficits
Section 8

Visual Field Evaluation

  • Confrontation testing (screening)
  • Automated perimetry (e.g., Humphrey) for detailed mapping
  • Identify scotomas or generalized field loss
Section 9

Fundoscopic Examination

  • Acute ON often normal disc (retrobulbar neuritis)
  • Possible disc swelling (papillitis)
  • Chronic: optic disc pallor indicates atrophy
Section 9

Neurological Examination

  • Cranial Nerves: check EOMs (III, IV, VI), CN V, etc.
  • Motor: weakness, spasticity
  • Sensory: pinprick, proprioception
  • Coordination: ataxia tests; Gait: spastic or unsteady gait
Section 10

MRI of the Brain & Orbits (1)

  • Purpose: Identify CNS demyelinating lesions
  • Optic Nerve Enhancement: T1 + gadolinium → active inflammation
  • T2/FLAIR: Hyperintensities showing older/new plaques
Section 10

MRI of the Brain & Orbits (2)

  • High MS Risk: Multiple lesions, especially periventricular
  • Optic Nerve Findings: Enhancement, swelling in acute ON
Section 10

Visual Evoked Potentials (VEP)

  • Measures conduction speed from retina to occipital cortex
  • Prolonged P100 latency indicates demyelination
  • Useful for subclinical lesions, supports MS diagnosis
Section 10

Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT)

  • Cross-sectional imaging of the retinal nerve fiber layer (RNFL)
  • Thinning of RNFL indicates axonal loss after ON
  • Helps monitor disease progression or treatment response
Section 11

Additional Laboratory Tests

  • Serologic: NMO-IgG (AQP4), MOG-IgG
  • Basic Labs: CBC, ESR, CRP, ANA (rule out systemic causes)
  • CSF: Oligoclonal bands supportive of MS
Section 12

Major Differentials

  • Neuromyelitis Optica (NMO)
  • MOG Antibody Disease
  • Ischemic Optic Neuropathy
  • Leber’s Hereditary Optic Neuropathy
  • Infectious Optic Neuritis (e.g., syphilis, Lyme)
  • Sarcoid/Autoimmune ON
Section 12

Clues to Differentials

  • NMO: Severe, bilateral, spinal cord lesions (longitudinally extensive)
  • MOG-ON: Disc swelling, often good recovery
  • Ischemic ON: Painless, altitudinal defect, older patients
  • Leber’s: Gradual bilateral central loss, maternal inheritance
  • Infectious: Systemic signs, positive serologies
Section 13

General Approach to Management

  • Confirm diagnosis: Clinical + MRI (+/- VEP)
  • Assess MS risk: Brain MRI for demyelinating lesions
  • Exclude atypical causes: NMO-IgG, MOG-IgG if suspicion
Section 13

High-Dose Corticosteroids

  • First-line for acute ON: IV methylprednisolone 1 g/day x 3–5 days
  • Oral steroids may follow IV or be used if mild
  • Speeds recovery but doesn’t always alter long-term final VA
Section 13

Disease-Modifying Therapies (DMTs)

  • Indicated if MS or high MS risk is confirmed
  • Examples:
    • Interferon β-1a/b
    • Glatiramer acetate
    • Dimethyl fumarate
    • Natalizumab, Ocrelizumab, etc.
  • Reduce relapse frequency & slow progression
Section 13

Other Immunomodulatory Therapies

  • NMO: Steroids + possible plasmapheresis; long-term rituximab or similar
  • MOG-ON: Steroids, IVIG, or other immunosuppressants depending on severity
  • Goal: Address underlying autoimmune process
Section 13

Plasma Exchange (Plasmapheresis)

  • Indicated for steroid-refractory acute ON
  • Removes circulating autoantibodies and immune complexes
  • May improve vision in refractory demyelinating ON
Section 14

Symptomatic Management

  • Pain control: NSAIDs, acetaminophen
  • Vision aids: large print, contrast enhancements
  • Cooling strategies: to mitigate Uhthoff’s phenomenon
Section 14

Natural Course of ON

  • Partial recovery often starts in 2–4 weeks, continues for months
  • Generally good final visual outcome in typical ON
  • Mild residual color or contrast deficits can persist
Section 14

Risk of Progression to MS

  • High lesion load on MRI = higher MS risk
  • Monitor with repeat exams & MRIs if new symptoms arise
  • Early DMT may delay or reduce MS progression
Section 14

Optic Neuritis Treatment Trial (ONTT)

  • Landmark trial: steroids vs. placebo in acute ON
  • IV steroids hastened recovery but no major long-term VA difference
  • Oral steroids alone had higher short-term recurrence
  • MRI lesions predicted higher MS risk
Section 15

Pediatric Optic Neuritis

  • Often bilateral in children
  • May follow viral infection or vaccination
  • High-dose steroids; evaluate for demyelinating disease
Section 15

Pregnancy and ON/MS

  • MS relapses may decrease during pregnancy, rebound postpartum
  • Some DMTs contraindicated in pregnancy; adjust therapy accordingly
  • Short-term IV steroids are generally safe if needed
Section 15

Emerging Therapies

  • Neuroprotective agents: Aiming to protect axons in acute inflammation
  • Remyelinating therapies: Enhance repair of myelin sheath
  • Biologics: New monoclonal antibodies targeting specific immune pathways
Section 16

Patient Education & Support

  • Explain ON & MS link, possible disease course
  • Lifestyle: Avoid smoking, ensure Vitamin D, manage stress
  • Resources: MS societies, vision rehab programs, counseling
Section 16

Key Takeaways

  • ON: acute unilateral vision loss, eye pain, color desaturation
  • Often first sign of MS; ~50% risk of MS over 15 years
  • Diagnosis: history, exam (RAPD, color vision, VF), MRI, VEP
  • Management: High-dose IV steroids, DMT if MS risk; generally good recovery
