Section 1: Introduction

Definition of Optic Neuritis

  • Optic Neuritis (ON): Inflammatory demyelination of the optic nerve
  • Presentation: Subacute onset of unilateral vision loss, eye pain
  • Significance: Often the first manifestation of MS

Section 1: Introduction

Importance of Studying ON in MS

  • ON is often the first demyelinating event in MS
  • ~15–20% of MS patients initially present with ON
  • Insight into autoimmune demyelination in the CNS
  • Early detection can prompt disease-modifying therapies

Section 2: Epidemiology & Risk Factors

Epidemiology

  • Age: Typically 20–40 years
  • Gender: Female predominance (~3:1)
  • Geographic Variation: Higher incidence farther from the equator
  • Association: 50% of isolated ON develop MS within 15 years

Section 2: Epidemiology & Risk Factors

Risk Factors

  • Genetic Predisposition: Certain HLA genotypes
  • Environmental: Low Vitamin D, smoking, possible viral triggers (EBV)
  • Autoimmune Disorders: May predispose to ON

Section 3: Pathophysiology

Basic Immunopathology

  • Autoimmune Attack: T-cells, B-cells, and antibodies target myelin
  • Key Cells: CD4+, CD8+ T cells, B cells, macrophages
  • Inflammatory Cascade: Cytokines (IL-2, IFN-γ) promote demyelination, axonal injury

Section 3: Pathophysiology

Demyelination in the Optic Nerve

  • Myelin Loss: Slows nerve conduction
  • Axonal Injury: Potentially permanent if severe
  • Remyelination: Explains partial or full visual recovery

Section 3: Pathophysiology

Role of MS Plaques

  • MS Plaques: Focal CNS demyelination
  • Locations: Periventricular, juxtacortical, infratentorial, spinal cord
  • MRI: T2/FLAIR lesions; active lesions enhance with gadolinium
  • Optic Nerve Lesions: Enhancement indicates acute inflammation

Section 4: Types of Optic Neuritis

Typical vs. Atypical ON

  • Typical (Demyelinating) ON:
    • Associated with MS
    • Usually unilateral, painful, good recovery
    • MRI often shows MS-type lesions
  • Atypical ON:
    • Causes: NMO, MOG, infections, sarcoidosis
    • Often bilateral or severe; may have worse prognosis

Section 4: Types of Optic Neuritis

Clinical Clues to Atypical ON

  • Poor steroid response
  • Recurrent bilateral involvement
  • Severe vision loss at onset
  • Systemic symptoms (rash, arthralgias)
  • Marked disc swelling on imaging

Section 5: Relationship Between ON & MS

ON as an Early Indicator of MS

  • 15–20% of MS patients present with ON initially
  • ~50% develop MS within 15 years after first ON
  • MRI brain lesions greatly increase MS conversion risk

Section 5: Relationship Between ON & MS

MS Diagnostic Criteria (Brief)

  • McDonald Criteria: Dissemination in time & space
  • Clinical Attacks + MRI Evidence:
    • ≥2 CNS regions (space)
    • Lesions at different times (time)
  • ON can fulfill part of the clinical requirement

Section 6: Clinical Features

Vision Loss

  • Nature: Sudden/subacute (~days to 1 week)
  • Severity: Mild blurring to complete loss
  • Laterality: Typically unilateral in demyelinating ON
  • Progression: Worsens ~1 week, then stabilizes/improves

Section 6: Clinical Features

Pain with Eye Movement

  • ~90% report retro-orbital pain
  • Pain worsens with movement
  • Linked to inflammation of optic nerve sheath

Section 6: Clinical Features

Color Vision Defects

  • Red desaturation is classic
  • Ishihara plates often detect color discrimination issues
  • Residual color deficits may persist after acuity recovers

Section 6: Clinical Features

Visual Field Defects

  • Central or cecocentral scotoma common
  • Diffuse or altitudinal deficits are less frequent
  • Confrontation (screen), automated perimetry (detailed)

Section 6: Clinical Features

Uhthoff’s Phenomenon

  • Temporary worsening of symptoms with heat/exercise
  • Raised temperature impairs conduction in demyelinated fibers
  • Can transiently reduce visual acuity

Section 6: Clinical Features

Relative Afferent Pupillary Defect (RAPD)

  • Detected by swinging flashlight test
  • Indicates unilateral/asymmetric optic nerve dysfunction
  • More pronounced RAPD = more severe ON

Section 7: History Taking

Key Points (Part 1)

  • Chief complaint: onset & nature of vision loss
  • Pain: especially with eye movement
  • Timeline: duration, progression
  • Possible triggers: infection, vaccination, stress

Section 7: History Taking

Key Points (Part 2)

  • Systemic Review:
    • Neurological: numbness, weakness, ataxia
    • Urinary changes, diplopia
  • Past Medical History:
    • Prior ON episodes
    • Autoimmune/neuro disorders
  • Family History: MS or other autoimmune diseases

Section 7: History Taking

Additional History Details

  • Medication use: steroids, immunomodulators
  • Social history: smoking, alcohol, occupation
  • Travel history: potential infections
  • Red flags: fever, weight loss, etc.

Section 8: Clinical Examination

Visual Acuity Testing

  • Check best-corrected VA (Snellen, ETDRS)
  • Compare with contralateral eye
  • Note any pinhole improvement

Section 8: Clinical Examination

Pupillary Examination

  • Size, shape, symmetry
  • Direct & consensual light reflex
  • Swinging flashlight test (RAPD)
  • Near response (accommodation, convergence)

Section 8: Clinical Examination

Color Vision Testing

  • Ishihara plates for color discrimination
  • Red desaturation test for subtle deficits
  • Document severity & changes over time

Section 8: Clinical Examination

Visual Field Evaluation

  • Confrontation testing (screen)
  • Automated perimetry (Humphrey) for detail
  • Identify scotomas or generalized field loss

Section 8: Clinical Examination

Fundoscopic Examination

  • Retrobulbar neuritis: normal disc in acute ON
  • Possible disc swelling (papillitis)
  • Chronic disc pallor = optic atrophy

Section 8: Clinical Examination

Neurological Examination

  • Cranial nerves: EOM (III, IV, VI), trigeminal (V), etc.
  • Motor: weakness, spasticity
  • Sensory: pinprick, proprioception
  • Coordination/gait: check for ataxia, spastic gait

Section 9: Investigations

MRI of the Brain & Orbits (1)

  • Purpose: Identify CNS demyelinating lesions
  • Optic Nerve Enhancement: T1 + gadolinium → active inflammation
  • T2/FLAIR: Hyperintensities showing older/new plaques

Section 9: Investigations

MRI of the Brain & Orbits (2)

  • High MS Risk: Multiple lesions, especially periventricular
  • Optic Nerve Findings: Enhancement, swelling in acute ON

Section 9: Investigations

Visual Evoked Potentials (VEP)

  • Measures conduction latency from retina to occipital cortex
  • Prolonged P100 = demyelination
  • Helps detect subclinical lesions, supports MS diagnosis

Section 9: Investigations

Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT)

  • Cross-sectional imaging of retinal nerve fiber layer (RNFL)
  • Thinning indicates axonal loss post-ON
  • Monitors disease progression or treatment response

Section 9: Investigations

Additional Laboratory Tests

  • Serologic: NMO-IgG (AQP4), MOG-IgG
  • Basic Labs: CBC, ESR, CRP, ANA (exclude other causes)
  • CSF: Oligoclonal bands supportive of MS

Section 10: Differential Diagnosis

Major Differentials

  • Neuromyelitis Optica (NMO)
  • MOG Antibody Disease
  • Ischemic Optic Neuropathy
  • Leber’s Hereditary Optic Neuropathy
  • Infectious ON (e.g., syphilis, Lyme)
  • Sarcoid/Autoimmune Optic Neuritis

Section 10: Differential Diagnosis

Clues to Different Etiologies

  • NMO: Severe, bilateral, spinal cord lesions
  • MOG-ON: Disc swelling, often good recovery
  • Ischemic ON: Painless, altitudinal defect, older patients
  • Leber’s: Gradual bilateral central loss, maternal inheritance
  • Infectious: Systemic signs, positive serologies

Section 11: Management

General Approach

  • Confirm diagnosis: clinical + MRI ± VEP
  • Assess MS risk: Brain MRI for lesions
  • Exclude atypical (NMO, MOG) if suspicion

Section 11: Management

High-Dose Corticosteroids

  • First-line: IV methylprednisolone 1 g/day x 3–5 days
  • Oral steroids may follow or be used if mild
  • Speeds recovery but may not alter final VA outcome

Section 11: Management

Disease-Modifying Therapies (DMTs)

  • Indicated if MS or high risk of MS
  • Examples:
    • Interferon β, Glatiramer, Dimethyl fumarate
    • Natalizumab, Ocrelizumab, etc.
  • Reduce relapse rate & slow progression

Section 11: Management

Other Immunomodulatory Therapies

  • NMO: Steroids, plasmapheresis, rituximab for maintenance
  • MOG-ON: Steroids ± IVIG or immunosuppressants
  • Target underlying autoimmune mechanisms

Section 11: Management

Plasma Exchange (Plasmapheresis)

  • Used for steroid-refractory acute ON
  • Removes autoantibodies/immune complexes
  • May improve vision in otherwise refractory ON

Section 11: Management

Symptomatic Management

  • Pain control: NSAIDs, acetaminophen
  • Vision aids: large print, contrast enhancements
  • Cooling strategies to reduce Uhthoff’s phenomenon

Section 12: Prognosis & Follow-Up

Natural Course of ON

  • Partial recovery often starts 2–4 weeks post-onset
  • Many have good final VA in typical ON
  • Color/contrast deficits may persist

Section 12: Prognosis & Follow-Up

Risk of Progression to MS

  • High lesion load on MRI → greater risk
  • Close monitoring with repeat neuro exams & MRI if new symptoms
  • Early DMT can delay or reduce MS progression

Section 12: Prognosis & Follow-Up

Optic Neuritis Treatment Trial (ONTT)

  • Landmark trial: steroids vs. placebo in acute ON
  • IV steroids hastened recovery but no major long-term VA difference
  • Oral steroids alone had higher short-term recurrence
  • MRI lesions predicted higher MS risk

Section 13: Special Considerations

Pediatric Optic Neuritis

  • Often bilateral in children
  • May follow viral infection or vaccination
  • High-dose steroids; evaluate for demyelinating disease

Section 13: Special Considerations

Pregnancy & ON/MS

  • MS relapses may decrease during pregnancy, rebound postpartum
  • Some DMTs contraindicated; therapy must be adjusted
  • Short-term IV steroids are generally safe if needed

Section 13: Future Directions

Emerging Therapies

  • Neuroprotective agents to preserve axons
  • Remyelinating therapies to enhance repair
  • Biologics: advanced monoclonal antibodies targeting key immune pathways

Section 13: Special Considerations

Patient Education & Support

  • Explain ON–MS link & typical disease course
  • Lifestyle: avoid smoking, optimize Vitamin D, manage stress
  • Resources: MS societies, vision rehab, counseling

Section 14: Conclusion

Key Takeaways

  • ON: unilateral vision loss, eye pain, color desaturation
  • Often the first sign of MS (50% risk over ~15 years)
  • Diagnose via clinical exam, MRI, VEP
  • Treat with high-dose IV steroids, consider DMT for MS risk
  • Generally favorable visual recovery in typical ON
