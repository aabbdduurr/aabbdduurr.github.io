Slide 3: Definition of Optic Neuritis

  • Optic Neuritis (ON): Inflammatory demyelination of the optic nerve.
  • Common Presentation: Subacute onset of unilateral vision loss and eye pain.
  • Significance: Frequently the initial manifestation of MS.

Slide 4: Importance of Studying ON in MS

  • ON is often the first demyelinating event in MS.
  • ~15–20% of MS patients initially present with ON.
  • Provides insight into autoimmune demyelination in the CNS.
  • Early detection can prompt disease-modifying therapies.

Slide 5: Epidemiology

  • Age: Common in young adults (20–40 years).
  • Gender: Female predominance (~3:1).
  • Geographic Variation: Higher incidence further from the equator.
  • Association: ~50% of isolated ON patients develop MS within 15 years.

Slide 6: Risk Factors

  • Genetic Predisposition: Certain HLA genotypes.
  • Environmental Factors:
    • Low Vitamin D
    • Smoking
    • Viral triggers (EBV association)
  • Autoimmune Disorders: Additional autoimmune diseases may predispose to ON.

Slide 7: Basic Immunopathology

  • Autoimmune Attack: T-cells, B-cells, and antibodies target myelin.
  • Key Immune Cells: T lymphocytes (CD4+, CD8+), B cells, macrophages.
  • Inflammatory Cascade: Cytokines (IL-2, IFN-γ) perpetuate demyelination and axonal injury.

Slide 8: Demyelination in the Optic Nerve

  • Myelin Sheath Destruction: Slows nerve conduction.
  • Axonal Injury: Potential for permanent damage.
  • Remyelination: Explains partial or full visual recovery.

Slide 9: Role of MS Plaques

  • MS Plaques: Focal areas of CNS demyelination.
  • Location: Periventricular, juxtacortical, infratentorial, spinal cord.
  • MRI Correlation: T2/FLAIR hyperintensities; active lesions enhance with gadolinium.
  • Optic Nerve Lesions: Enhancement indicates acute inflammation in ON.

Slide 10: Typical vs. Atypical ON

  • Typical ON (Demyelinating):
    • Associated with MS
    • Unilateral, painful, better recovery
    • MRI often shows MS-like lesions
  • Atypical ON:
    • Causes: NMO, MOG disease, infections, sarcoidosis
    • Often bilateral or severe, can have worse prognosis

Slide 11: Clinical Clues to Atypical ON

  • Poor response to steroids
  • Recurrent bilateral involvement
  • Severe vision loss at onset
  • Systemic symptoms (e.g., rash, arthralgias)
  • Marked disc swelling on imaging

Slide 12: ON as an Early Indicator of MS

  • First symptom in 15–20% of MS patients
  • ~50% develop MS within 15 years after first ON
  • MRI brain lesions = higher MS conversion risk

Slide 13: MS Diagnostic Criteria (Brief)

  • McDonald Criteria: Dissemination in time & space
  • Clinical Attacks + MRI Evidence:
    • Dissemination in Space: ≥2 CNS regions
    • Dissemination in Time: Lesions at different times
  • Optic Neuritis helps fulfill clinical criteria

Slide 14: Vision Loss

  • Nature: Sudden or subacute onset over days to 1-2 weeks
  • Severity: Ranges from mild blurring to complete vision loss
  • Laterality: Usually unilateral in demyelinating ON
  • Progression: Worsens ~1 week, then stabilizes/improves

Slide 15: Pain with Eye Movement

  • Occurs in ~90% of ON patients
  • Retro-orbital pain worsened by eye movement
  • Due to inflammation of optic nerve sheath

Slide 16: Color Vision Defects

  • Red desaturation is a hallmark
  • Ishihara plates may show color discrimination deficits
  • Persisting color vision changes even after VA improves

Slide 17: Visual Field Defects

  • Central or cecocentral scotoma most common
  • Diffuse field loss or altitudinal defect (less common)
  • Confrontation testing (basic), automated perimetry (detailed)

Slide 18: Uhthoff’s Phenomenon

  • Worsening of neuro symptoms with heat/exercise
  • Elevated temperature impairs conduction in demyelinated fibers
  • May transiently reduce visual acuity

Slide 19: Relative Afferent Pupillary Defect (RAPD)

  • Checked via the swinging flashlight test
  • Indicates unilateral/asymmetric optic nerve dysfunction
  • More pronounced = more severe ON involvement

Slide 20: Key Points in History (Part 1)

  • Chief Complaint: nature & onset of vision loss
  • Pain: especially with eye movement
  • Timeline: duration, progression
  • Triggers: recent infection, vaccination, stress

Slide 21: Key Points in History (Part 2)

  • Systemic Review:
    • Neurological symptoms: numbness, weakness, ataxia
    • Urinary incontinence, diplopia
  • Past Medical History:
    • Previous ON episodes
    • Autoimmune or neurological disorders
  • Family History: MS, autoimmune diseases

Slide 22: Additional History Details

  • Medication Use: steroids, immunomodulators
  • Social History: smoking, alcohol, occupation
  • Travel History: consider infections
  • Red Flags: systemic symptoms like fever, weight loss

Slide 23: Visual Acuity Testing

  • Snellen or ETDRS chart for best-corrected VA
  • Record findings and compare with contralateral eye
  • Check for pinhole improvement

Slide 24: Pupillary Examination

  • Check size, shape, symmetry
  • Direct and consensual light reflex
  • Swinging flashlight test (RAPD)
  • Near reflex (accommodation, convergence)

Slide 25: Color Vision Testing

  • Ishihara plates
  • Red desaturation test (compare intensity in each eye)
  • Document severity or presence of color deficits

Slide 26: Visual Field Evaluation

  • Confrontation test (screening)
  • Automated perimetry (Humphrey, etc.) for detailed mapping
  • Identify scotomas or general field loss

Slide 27: Fundoscopic Examination

  • Acute ON: often normal disc (retrobulbar neuritis)
  • Possible disc swelling (papillitis)
  • Chronic: optic disc pallor (atrophy)

Slide 28: Neurological Examination

  • Cranial Nerves: check extraocular movements, CN V, etc.
  • Motor Exam: weakness, spasticity
  • Sensory Exam: pinprick, proprioception
  • Coordination, Gait: look for ataxia or spastic gait

Slide 29: MRI of the Brain & Orbits (Part 1)

  • Purpose: Identify CNS demyelinating lesions
  • Optic Nerve Enhancement: T1 with gadolinium indicates inflammation
  • T2/FLAIR Hyperintensities: Old or new MS plaques

Slide 30: MRI of the Brain & Orbits (Part 2)

  • High-Risk for MS:
    • Multiple brain lesions
    • Periventricular distribution
  • Optic Nerve Findings: Enhancement, swelling in acute phase

Slide 31: Visual Evoked Potentials (VEP)

  • Measures electrical conduction from retina to occipital cortex
  • Prolonged P100 latency = demyelination
  • Useful in detecting subclinical lesions

Slide 32: Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT)

  • Cross-sectional imaging of retinal nerve fiber layer (RNFL)
  • Thinning of RNFL indicates axonal loss post-ON
  • Useful to monitor progression or treatment response

Slide 33: Additional Laboratory Tests

  • Serologic Tests: NMO-IgG, MOG-IgG
  • Basic Labs: CBC, ESR, CRP, ANA (to exclude systemic causes)
  • CSF Analysis: Oligoclonal bands supportive of MS

Slide 34: Major Differentials

  • Neuromyelitis Optica (NMO)
  • MOG Antibody Disease
  • Ischemic Optic Neuropathy
  • Leber’s Hereditary Optic Neuropathy
  • Infectious Optic Neuritis (syphilis, Lyme)
  • Sarcoid/Autoimmune ON

Slide 35: Clues to Differentials

  • NMO: Severe, often bilateral, spinal cord lesions
  • MOG-ON: Possible disc swelling, often good recovery
  • Ischemic ON: Painless, altitudinal field loss, older pts
  • Leber’s Hereditary: Gradual bilateral loss, maternal inheritance
  • Infectious: Systemic signs, positive serologies

Slide 36: General Approach to Management

  • Confirm diagnosis: Clinical + MRI (+/- VEP)
  • Assess MS risk: Brain MRI lesions
  • Exclude atypical causes: NMO-IgG, MOG-IgG if indicated

Slide 37: High-Dose Corticosteroids

  • First-line for acute ON: IV methylprednisolone 1 g/day x 3–5 days
  • Oral steroids can follow IV or be used in mild cases
  • Speeds recovery but may not alter final visual outcome significantly

Slide 38: Disease-Modifying Therapies (DMTs) in MS

  • Indicated if MS or high risk of MS is diagnosed
  • Examples:
    • Interferon β-1a/b
    • Glatiramer acetate
    • Dimethyl fumarate
    • Natalizumab, Ocrelizumab, etc.
  • Reduce relapse rate & slow progression

Slide 39: Other Immunomodulatory Therapies

  • NMO: Steroids + plasmapheresis; long-term: rituximab, etc.
  • MOG-ON: Steroids, IVIG, or other immunosuppressants
  • Goal: Address underlying autoimmune process

Slide 40: Plasma Exchange (Plasmapheresis)

  • Indication: Steroid-refractory acute ON
  • Removes autoantibodies and immune complexes
  • May improve vision in otherwise refractory cases

Slide 41: Symptomatic Management

  • Pain control: NSAIDs, acetaminophen
  • Vision aids: large print, contrast enhancements
  • Cooling strategies for Uhthoff’s phenomenon

Slide 42: Natural Course of ON

  • Partial recovery in 2–4 weeks, continues over months
  • Good long-term visual prognosis in typical ON
  • Mild residual defects possible (especially color vision)

Slide 43: Risk of Progression to MS

  • MRI lesion load correlates with higher risk
  • Ongoing neurological exams & repeat MRIs if new symptoms
  • Early DMT may reduce or delay MS progression

Slide 44: Optic Neuritis Treatment Trial (ONTT)

  • Landmark trial comparing steroids vs. placebo
  • IV steroids hastened recovery but no major long-term VA difference
  • Oral steroids alone had higher short-term recurrence
  • MRI lesions predicted higher MS risk

Slide 45: Pediatric Optic Neuritis

  • Often bilateral in children
  • May follow viral infection or vaccination
  • Treat with high-dose steroids, evaluate for underlying demyelination

Slide 46: Pregnancy and ON/MS

  • MS relapses may decrease during pregnancy, rebound postpartum
  • DMT adjustments needed (some contraindicated in pregnancy)
  • Steroids short-term generally safe if indicated

Slide 47: Emerging Therapies

  • Neuroprotective Agents: to protect axons during acute inflammation
  • Remyelinating Therapies: promote myelin repair
  • Biologics: advanced monoclonal antibodies targeting immune pathways

Slide 48: Patient Education & Support

  • Explain ON & MS link, disease course
  • Lifestyle: avoid smoking, ensure Vitamin D, manage stress
  • Resources: MS societies, vision rehab programs, counseling

Slide 49: Key Takeaways

  • ON: acute unilateral vision loss, eye pain, color desaturation
  • Often first sign of MS; ~50% risk over 15 years
  • Diagnosis: clinical exam (RAPD, color vision, VF), MRI, VEP
  • Management: High-dose IV steroids, possible DMTs, good recovery typical
